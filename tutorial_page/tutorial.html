12 Principles of Animation

Let motion do the talking! Master these 12 timeless principles to bring your animations to life with personality and flair.

1. Squash and Stretch

Give weight and flexibility to your objects. When something jumps or falls, compress it (squash) on impact and expand it (stretch) during movement. This principle makes rigid objects feel alive and flexible!

2. Anticipation

Prepare the audience for action before it happens. A character crouches before jumping, pulls back before punching, or looks toward an object before picking it up. This builds tension and makes actions feel natural.

3. Staging

Direct the viewer's attention clearly. Use camera angles, lighting, positioning, and color to guide focus to the important action. Good staging tells the story without confusing the audience.

4. Straight Ahead Action and Pose to Pose

Two approaches to animation: Straight Ahead draws frame-by-frame continuously, while Pose to Pose plans key poses first, then fills in between frames. Mix both techniques for the best results!

5. Follow Through and Overlapping Action

Different body parts stop moving at different times. When a character stops running, their torso stops first, but their hair, cape, or arms keep moving. This creates fluid, realistic motion.

6. Slow In and Slow Out

Objects accelerate when starting and decelerate when stopping. Add more frames near the beginning and end of a movement to create easing. This makes animation feel natural instead of mechanical.

7. Arc

Most natural motion follows a curved path, not a straight line. An arm swinging, a ball flying through the air, or a character jumping all follow arcs. This adds grace and realism to movement.

8. Secondary Action

Add supporting movements that complement the main action. While a character talks, they might gesture with their hands or shift their weight. These details bring personality and depth to your animation.

9. Timing

Control the number of frames for each action to convey weight, emotion, and impact. A slow, deliberate movement feels different from a quick, snappy one. Perfect your timing to nail the performance!

10. Exaggeration

Push movements beyond reality for dramatic effect. In cartoons, expressions are bigger, jumps are higher, and reactions are more extreme. Exaggeration keeps animation entertaining and expressive!

11. Solid Drawing

Create characters and objects with proper form, structure, and dimensionality. Strong drawing skills are the foundation of great animation. Understand anatomy, perspective, and proportion to make compelling designs.

12. Appeal

Make your characters and animation engaging and likeable. Good appeal comes from appealing designs, clear movement, and personality. Whether cute, cool, or charismatic, characters should captivate the audience!
